The Wall of Money

An In-depth Analysis of China's Liquidity Conundrum: Why a Flood of Money Has Failed to Irrigate the Real Economy.

A Tale of Two Monies: The Great Decoupling of M1 & M2

To understand China's monetary predicament, one must begin by analyzing the startling and persistent divergence between the growth rates of broad money (M2) and narrow money (M1). This phenomenon, known as the "scissors gap," is the most potent single indicator of the economy's underlying pathology. It acts like an economic MRI, clearly imaging the structural fracture between the financial system and the real economy.

What are M1 and M2?

In the Chinese context, M2 represents the total potential purchasing power in the economy. M1, however, composed mainly of corporate demand deposits, is a critical "thermometer" of business vitality. When M1 growth stagnates or contracts, it signals that firms are pulling back from the front lines of production and investment.

A Historical Warning

The current scissors gap, which began in late 2021, is more complex and severe than previous episodes. It coincided with the deep correction in the property sector and a sweeping regulatory crackdown on platform and education industries, reflecting a structural collapse in private sector confidence.

The Micro-Mechanism: How Money Gets "Frozen"

Behind the scissors gap are the rational choices of countless micro-agents. Faced with a pessimistic outlook on future demand and low returns on physical investment, firms prefer to park their loans in term deposits or wealth management products. This behavior of "risk-free arbitrage" or "precautionary saving" causes newly created money to be immediately "frozen," manifesting as a "liquidity trap" on a macro level.

The Three Gorges of Capital

The vast ocean of money has not vanished. Instead, it has been intercepted by three colossal dams, trapped in inefficient cycles. These dams are interconnected, jointly forming a liquidity trap with Chinese characteristics. In the following three pages, we will dissect the structure, causes, and profound impact of each dam.

The Private Sector's Great Retreat

First Dam: The State Siphon

This is the most uniquely Chinese component of the liquidity trap. Whereas credit in market economies is primarily demand-driven, in China, it is largely supply-driven by policy. The vast majority of new credit is directed, by state command, to State-Owned Enterprises (SOEs) and Local Government Financing Vehicles (LGFVs).

This capital is used for two main purposes: funding large-scale infrastructure projects with diminishing returns to meet GDP targets, and, to a greater extent, rolling over maturing debt. This creates a massive internal capital loop that, while maintaining financial stability on paper, locks capital in low- or even negative-return projects and severely crowds out the more dynamic private sector.

Second Dam: The Property Quagmire

The property sector, once the engine pulling nearly a third of China's economy, has now devolved into a capital-devouring quagmire. After decades of a credit-fueled boom, the industry is facing a historic reckoning. Liquidity is not flowing to value-creating new projects but is being used in a costly defensive battle.

The core policy objective is "ensuring the delivery of pre-sold homes" to maintain social stability. To this end, the government has set up special loans and developer "whitelists," allowing only selected, mostly state-owned, developers to receive new financing. This capital is essentially "life support," used to patch up huge balance sheet holes and complete "zombie projects," not for market-driven investment. This is a classic value-destroying process.

Third Dam: The Private Sector's Great Retreat

This is the final loop of the liquidity trap. Facing policy uncertainty, persistent deflationary pressures, and a bleak outlook, China's once-vibrant private sectorâ€”both firms and householdsâ€”is staging an unprecedented "great retreat." Their core behavior has shifted from "profit maximization" to "balance sheet repair" and "risk aversion."

At the corporate level, this manifests as an "investment strike" and active debt repayment. At the household level, it appears as "excess savings" and consumption downgrades. The fall in property prices has severely eroded household wealth, and this, combined with anxieties about future employment and income, has prompted households to save a larger portion of their income. This collective conservative behavior has led to a contraction in aggregate demand, further worsening the business environment and creating a vicious cycle.

Policy Echo Chamber: Official Tone & Academic Debate

The Strategic Resolve of Top Leadership

The core guiding principle has been to **"seek progress while maintaining stability, establish the new before abolishing the old."** This carefully worded phrase precisely outlines the leadership's policy preferences and bottom-line thinking.

"Stability" is the overriding prerequisite. This means that in tackling thorny issues like local debt and real estate, policymakers are keen to avoid "shock therapy" that could trigger violent market turmoil. Instead, they prefer a gradual, cautious approach to defuse risks over time.

"Progress" is embodied in the emphasis on "new quality productive forces."** This concept signals a strategic shift in development focusâ€”away from traditional real estate and infrastructure towards state-led, tech-driven advanced manufacturing, such as semiconductors, AI, and new energy.

The PBOC's Policy Rhetoric

The People's Bank of China (PBOC) has been trying to shift the market's focus from the sheer **"quantity"** of credit growth to the **"quality"** and **"efficiency"** of its structure.

"It is necessary to revitalize financial resources that are inefficiently occupied and improve the efficiency of capital use."

This narrative shift is a tacit admission of the diminishing returns of the "flood-like irrigation" model and reflects the difficulty of boosting credit aggregates amid weak private-sector demand. Meanwhile, statements that "our monetary policy toolkit remains ample" are intended to stabilize confidence.

The Fierce Debate Among Economists

Outside the cautious official tone, the academic debate is far more intense, roughly divided into two camps:

The **"Demand-Side" or "Stimulus" camp** argues that the main contradiction is insufficient aggregate demand and deflationary risk. They advocate for more decisive and expansionary fiscal and monetary policies, such as large-scale central government bond issuance to directly boost household consumption.

The **"Supply-Side" or "Reform" camp** is skeptical of further state-led stimulus. They argue that the root of the problem is resource misallocation and low investment efficiency. They contend that the policy focus should be on deepening market-oriented reforms to unleash the "animal spirits" of the private sector.

The Pathology: A Hybrid Crisis

The Demand Side: A Balance Sheet Recession

Richard Koo's "balance sheet recession" theory provides a powerful framework for understanding the behavior of China's private sector. Its core tenet is that after a major asset bubble bursts (in China's case, real estate), the primary goal of firms and households shifts from profit maximization to **debt minimization**.

This means that no matter how low interest rates go, economic agents will not borrow for new investment or consumption. Instead, they use all available cash flow to pay down existing debt. This collective deleveraging leads to a collapse in private credit demand, rendering monetary policy ineffectiveâ€”a classic "liquidity trap."

The Supply Side: Command-Economy Sclerosis

However, this theory doesn't explain everything. If the problem were merely a lack of private demand, massive government fiscal expansion should be an effective countermeasure. Yet, despite unprecedented government borrowing and infrastructure spending, China's economy remains sluggish. This is because it is also afflicted by a second, more unique malady: "command-economy sclerosis."

This "sclerosis" manifests in the credit supply channels. China's banking system fundamentally serves the state's will. Credit is allocated not based on market risk-return principles, but according to industrial policy and political directives. This means new liquidity is funneled preferentially to large SOEs and LGFVs, funding projects with low returns or simply rolling over bad debt. This practice stifles efficiency and nurtures "zombie firms."

Combined Diagnosis: A Vicious Cycle of Policy Paralysis

China's predicament is a vicious cycle created by the superposition of these two maladies. The balance sheet recession on the demand side means the private sector "won't borrow." The command-economy sclerosis on the supply side means banks "won't lend to the private sector." Monetary policy is thus doubly ineffective, unable to stimulate effective credit demand or control its efficient allocation.

Finale: Ripple Effects & The Difficult Way Out

Ripple Effects: The Price of Stagnation

Domestic Impact: A "Chronic Disease"

  • Threat of a Deflationary Spiral: Persistent negative PPI and sluggish CPI are creating self-fulfilling deflationary expectations, weighing on debt burdens and economic activity.
  • Cracks in the Social Contract: High youth unemployment is not just an economic problem but a profound social one, eroding the implicit social contract based on ever-improving living standards.
  • Financial System Fragility: Banks, especially smaller ones, face a squeeze from narrowing interest margins and rising bad loans, accumulating systemic financial risk.

Global Spillover: From Growth Engine to Risk Source

  • Exporting Deflation: To offload excess domestic capacity, China is exporting vast quantities of manufactured goods at competitive prices, sparking trade frictions with the West.
  • Weakening Global Demand: As the "world's factory" slows, its demand for commodities like iron ore, copper, and oil wanes, impacting resource-exporting nations.
  • Capital Outflow Pressure: A lack of domestic confidence and falling investment returns are fueling motivations for capital flight, putting pressure on the RMB and posing a risk to regional financial stability.

The Way Out: A Profound Strategic Shift

Faced with this hybrid crisis, any piecemeal policy adjustments within the existing framework will be futile. What China needs is a fundamental, multi-pronged strategic pivot that addresses the core of its economic model.

1. Heal the Private Sector: Rebuild the Social Safety Net

To eradicate the fear driving the private sector, China must build a robust, universal social safety net. Drastically improving pensions, healthcare, and unemployment benefits is the only way to structurally reduce precautionary savings and unleash consumption.

2. Restructure the State Sector: Perform "Surgery"

The continuous transfusion of capital to "zombie" firms and inefficient LGFVs must stop. A decisive, central government-led restructuring of local government and developer debt is unavoidable. This may require creating a national "bad bank" to carve out toxic assets from the financial systemâ€”a painful but necessary surgery.

3. Reshape Aggregate Demand: Target Households Directly

Fiscal policy must shift its focus from inefficient supply-side investments to directly stimulating household consumption. Instead of building more highways, this means implementing large-scale, direct fiscal transfers to households, whether through consumption vouchers, tax cuts, or even direct cash handouts, to bypass the clogged financial channels and break the deflationary spiral.
